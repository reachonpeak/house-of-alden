On Leather That Darkens

A strap has two kinds of surface. There is the face, which is skin, finished by the tannery long before it reached us. And there is the cut edge, which is not skin at all but a cross-section: the fibre of the hide, opened by a knife and left looking at the world. Everything below is about the second kind.

The trade's answer to a cut edge is paint. Several coats, laid in and burnished flat, and the result is genuinely handsome — a hard, sealed, glossy line, identical on every strap that leaves the bench. We understand the appeal. It is the quickest way to make a strap look expensive in a photograph.

The trouble arrives later. Edge paint is a film sitting on top of a material that bends, and a strap bends in the same two places several times a day for years. Films crack where they are asked to fold. A cracked coat lifts, a lifted coat flakes away, and what is left is worse than an edge nobody ever painted: a good strap with a scar down it. Most of us have owned that strap.

So we leave the edge undyed. Nothing is added. The cut is the finish.

On the first day this looks like a mistake, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. The edge sits pale against a tan face and reads as unfinished, because it is. Someone will assume we ran out of budget. It is the one part of our watches that looks less considered than it would on a cheaper one.

What happens afterwards is the whole argument. An open edge has nothing over it, so it takes on whatever it is nearest, and takes it on first: skin, air, sun, the oil off a thumb. It darkens ahead of the face, and not evenly, because nobody wears a strap evenly. The stretch under a shirt cuff is not the stretch that has spent a summer in the open, and the place your thumb sits when you unbuckle goes first and furthest. By the hundredth day the edge is not a colour but a record: it will tell you which hand you favour and which sleeve you push up.

Here we should say the obvious thing. We have not watched a hundred days happen to one of our own straps: we opened four days ago. What we are describing is what leather does — a subject older and better documented than this house — and what we have watched happen to other people's straps over many years. Our own hundredth day falls in October. When it comes we will show you a strap instead of telling you about one, and if it has done something other than this note promises, we will write that down too.

The point of dye is uniformity: every strap the same on the day it is made and kept that way as long as the dye can manage. That is a reasonable thing to want, and we have not made it. An undyed edge wants the opposite. It is the only part of what we sell that we deliberately leave for somebody else to finish — the somebody is you, the work takes about a year, and the wages are that nobody else's looks like yours.

One exception, in fairness. The Maur Field wears olive canvas and none of this applies to it. Canvas does not darken with any grace at all. It gets dirty, you wash it, and it is a canvas strap again. Some days that is the better argument.
