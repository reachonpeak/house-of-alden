Why We Chose the Moon

Let us make the case against it first, since it is the stronger one.

A moonphase is a complication — the trade's word for anything a watch does beyond telling the hour — and of all of them it is the hardest to defend. A chronograph times a thing. A date window saves you asking. A moonphase reports the age of the moon, a fact that is free, unowned and hanging directly above your head. It is the only instrument we can think of that duplicates something you could confirm by stepping outside.

It was useful once. Before the grid reached this part of Punjab, the moon settled whether a road was walkable after dark and how late you could leave. The lunar month runs 29 days, 12 hours and 44 minutes, and a person who knew where they stood in it knew what the night would do. Electric light ended that argument inside a generation, and the moonphase has been coasting on its dignity ever since.

We put one on the Regent anyway. Two reasons, and the first is local.

The moon has not retired from Maur Mandi. Puranmashi and Masya arrive when the moon says so and not when the calendar on the wall does. Karva Chauth ends when it rises and not a minute before, as anyone who has waited on a roof with a plate can tell you. In a town where a good share of the year's dates are still lunar, a watch that keeps the moon in a window is not quite a folly. It is nearly a calendar.

The second reason is not local at all. Everything else on a dial is a human arrangement agreeing with itself. The hour is a decision. Noon is a treaty. The date is administration. The moon is the only reading on the watch that nobody negotiated — it would keep to that same figure if every meeting on earth were cancelled tomorrow. A watch that tracks it concedes it is not the last word on time, which we find a restful quality in an object strapped to the wrist.

It follows that the thing must be set. That figure divides tidily into nothing, so a moonphase and the calendar part company slowly, by arithmetic rather than by fault, and sooner or later the disc wants correcting by hand. We would rather you had that from us now than found it out in a year.

The rest of the Regent follows the moon rather than leads it. The dial is midnight blue because a crescent needs a night to sit in. The case is gold-tone because the crescent is gilt and would be orphaned on steel. It is an evening watch, which is rather the point, and the most ornamental thing we make. It is also the piece we can least justify on paper, which is why it has taken us a page.

We have been trading for eleven days and there are four watches on our list. One of them, the Ward Slim, has a bare dial, two hands and nothing whatever to say for itself, and we stand behind it entirely. It seemed only fair that one of the four should go the other way and carry something you did not ask for, cannot use, and will look at anyway.

That is the defence. The moonphase does nothing. We would add only that doing nothing is not the same as being for nothing, and that most of what people keep for thirty years was, strictly speaking, unnecessary on the day it was bought.
