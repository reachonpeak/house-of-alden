A House in Maur Mandi

This is being written on the first day, which is the only day on which a house can describe itself completely and still be brief.

House of Alden is Kawalpreet Kaur and Parvinder Kaur. It was registered this morning as a micro-enterprise — UDYAM-PB-03-0079079, if you would like to look it up, and we would rather you looked it up than took our word for anything. Micro-enterprise is the government's term for two people and a room. In this instance the term is exact.

The room is at 1560, Ward No. 5, Near Dispensary, Maur Mandi, Block Maur, Bathinda, Punjab 151509. The dispensary in that line is not decoration; it is how anybody finds the place. Addresses here run on landmarks, and the state has gone to the trouble of giving ours one. Inside: a drawing table, the stock, a bench where things are packed, and the two of us. There is no second room, and nothing we intend to do requires one.

Behind all of it is a drawer of old jewellery. Most families here have one and ours is not remarkable — the ordinary run of such a drawer, which is to say hooks gone soft, a stud short of its back, plating worn through at exactly the spot a thumb sits.

The instructive thing about a drawer like that is what is not in it. The valuable pieces are elsewhere, in a locker, and nobody has looked at them since the last wedding. What survives in the drawer is what was worn. A thing is kept because it was carried, and the carried and the costly turn out to be almost separate sets. That is the whole of our thinking. We have not improved on it, and this shop is the first attempt to act on it.

So: watches and earrings. Nothing else, and nothing else planned. Two of us, two categories — arithmetic before philosophy. Both are objects a person puts on without deciding to, wears through an ordinary Tuesday, and stops noticing. Those are the things that end up in the drawer.

Two matters we should settle before anybody infers them.

The first is the name. It looks as though it came down from somebody. It did not — we chose it this year, and the crest at the top of this page is ours because it is new, not because it was handed over.

The second is the way this shop is dressed. It is got up as a nineteenth-century trade catalogue, with engraved rules and ornaments and a crown over the wordmark, and we know perfectly well what that suggests. It is not a claim. It is a preference about how we would like to be looked at while we work. The line beneath the wordmark on our front page reads Est. 2026, and it is not a misprint for 1926. We came from July.

There is no archive here. No founding decade. No craftsman with forty years in his hands whose name we could put to a piece. There is a short list, an order book that started empty this morning, and a telephone that sits in this room — Kawalpreet's, and she answers it herself.

We will add pieces slowly, because two people cannot add them any other way, and we will not add categories. When anything here changes it will be written down in this journal, since there is nowhere else for it to go and nobody employed to announce it.

That is day one. Come back in a while and check the arithmetic.
